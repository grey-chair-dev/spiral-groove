1. User Personas
🎧 Persona 1 — “The Collector”
Profile:

Age: 28–60

Gender: All

Occupation: Professionals, hobbyists, long-time vinyl enthusiasts

Income: $60K–120K

Location: Primarily Greater Cincinnati area, plus online buyers nationwide

Motivations:

Searching for specific records, pressings, or rare finds

Trustworthy descriptions and accurate grading

Supporting local record shops rather than anonymous online sellers

Pain Points:

Frustration with inaccurate online inventory

Overly cluttered or dated websites

Lack of clear condition information or pressing details

Decision Triggers:

Verified availability

Visible grading (VG+, NM, M)

Clear shipping policies and staff curation confidence

🎁 Persona 2 — “The Gifter”
Profile:

Age: 22–45

Motivation: Shopping for birthdays, anniversaries, holidays

Tech Literacy: Moderate

Purchase Behavior: Spontaneous, quick-decision buyer

Motivations:

Simple browsing experience with clear categories

Gift guides or featured recommendations

Aesthetic packaging and storytelling

Pain Points:

Unclear site navigation or overwhelming catalogs

No “gift-ready” or recommendation section

Difficulty checking availability quickly

Decision Triggers:

Clean, visual product layout

“Perfect for fans of…” descriptions

Easy checkout and gift card options

🎶 Persona 3 — “The Local Music Lover”
Profile:

Age: 18–40

Occupation: Students, artists, freelancers, musicians

Location: Local to Milford / Cincinnati

Behavior: Attends shows, follows local artists, supports small businesses

Motivations:

Wants to attend live events or rent event space

Seeks connection and community

Interested in store events, local bands, pop-up collaborations

Pain Points:

Event info buried or outdated

No clear call-to-action for inquiries

Lack of social proof (photos, testimonials)

Decision Triggers:

Event calendar visibility

Quick inquiry submission and fast response

Clear local presence (maps, photos, partners)

💻 Persona 4 — “The Online Crate Digger”
Profile:

Age: 20–45

Location: National (vinyl collector network)

Behavior: Compares multiple online shops before purchasing

Motivations:

Real-time inventory accuracy

Rare pressings or unique finds not on Discogs

Transparent condition info and shipping speed

Pain Points:

Disconnected stock data (website doesn’t match inventory)

Poor mobile UX or loading times

Lack of trust in smaller shop websites

Decision Triggers:

Verified inventory from POS sync (Square)

Secure checkout

Strong branding and photography for credibility

4. Modern UX/UI Trends
Relevant 2025 Best Practices for Retail & Music Sites:

Mobile-first Design: Vertical navigation, sticky cart, and dynamic filtering.

Live Inventory Feedback: API-driven accuracy (Square integration).

Emotional Storytelling: Lifestyle photography, human-focused brand narrative.

Quick Add-to-Cart UX: Modal preview + “Continue Shopping” option.

Inclusive Design: Font scaling, color contrast ≥ 4.5:1, dark/light mode.

Microinteractions: Gentle hover animations on records; smooth fade-ins for lists.

Personalized Recommendations: Auto-suggested “You Might Also Like” section.

Local SEO Structured Data: Google Events, Products, and Organization schema.

6. Brand Aesthetic & Visual Direction
🎨 Core Aesthetic
Theme: Warm Analog Revival — evokes cozy record store ambiance.

Lighting: Golden tones, string-light warmth, black matte accents.

Texture: Subtle grain, paper/fabric overlays for authenticity.

Color Palette:

Primary: Deep Charcoal (#111111), Cream White (#F5F3EE)

Accent: Muted Teal (#3E787C), Vintage Amber (#DDAA44)

✍️ Typography
Headings: Playfair Display (nostalgic yet elegant)

Body: Inter / Poppins (clean, approachable)

🖼️ Imagery
Real in-store photography preferred over stock.

Macro vinyl textures, candid staff shots, live music photos.

Include short videos or looping animations of records spinning for engagement.

🪞 Visual Identity Goals
Feel like “stepping into the shop” online.

Emphasize trust, warmth, and simplicity.

Avoid clutter, gimmicks, or overly digital effects.
